The African Face of The Migrant Crisis

Fleeing persecution and a lack of economic opportunities, sub-saharan migrants face a treacherous journey to the US, only to be confronted by mounting challenges


Mauritanian migrants protesting in front of the United Nations in New York, Sept. 20, 2023. Photo credit: Naoufal Enhari.

By Naoufal Enhari


November, 16, 2023


Elhadi Mohamed and his cousin Mohameden left their native Mauritania five months ago with one goal in mind - to reach the US southern border and head north to New York.

Their two weeks journey by air, sea, and land, took them through multiple countries across Central America, a migration route that has been attracting lately an increasing number of African migrants.

One nationality in particular has seen an exponential increase lately among individuals seeking asylum in the United States: Mauritanians.

The surge since last fall in the backlog of asylum cases filed by Mauritanian nationals in US and New York State immigration courts is indicative of the increase in the number of their arrivals.

In fiscal year 2023 - which runs from October 1, 2022, to September 30, 2023 - the number of backlogs in asylum cases for Mauritanians increased by nearly 800%, reaching 4,000 cases, from 515 cases a year before.


“The younger generations of West Africans are dreaming of America,” said Boukary Sawadogo, an associate professor at the City College of New York, who has researched and written about African immigration and diaspora in the United States.

In an interview, he argues that a growing number of young Africans are abandoning Europe as a migration destination in favor of the United States.
